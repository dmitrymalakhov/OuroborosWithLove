# HR Hiring Playbook

This playbook is a compact hiring standard distilled from Russian HR interview transcripts about hiring employees, delegation, first hires, interview mistakes, channels, motivation, values, and onboarding.

## Operating Principles

- A vacancy should be honest. Do not polish away real constraints: team stage, office, salary range, workload, uncertainty, and management style should be visible.
- Define the role before screening candidates: business task, expected outcomes, must-have skills, nice-to-have skills, sufficient competence level, compensation logic, and interview stages.
- Do not chase a "star" by default. For most roles the useful target is sufficient competence plus motivation, learning ability, values fit, and ability to deliver in this company context.
- Separate hard skills, soft skills, motivation, and values. Strong expertise does not compensate for toxic behavior that breaks the team.
- The interview should reveal the candidate, not only sell the company. Start with a calm setup, reduce stress, ask for evidence, and let the candidate show thinking and motivation.
- Build a wide funnel. Job boards are only one channel; Telegram job channels, professional communities, referrals, personal brand, and team brand often surface stronger candidates.
- Paid test tasks and probation are for validation, not cheap labor. Good candidates avoid exploitative testing and unrealistic probation terms.
- Hiring does not end at the offer. Onboarding, documents, first tasks, feedback cadence, and 30/60/90 checkpoints are part of the hiring quality.

## Vacancy Audit Checklist

- Is the role goal explicit: what business problem will the person solve?
- Are responsibilities concrete and outcome-based?
- Are requirements split into must-have and nice-to-have?
- Is the compensation realistic and transparent enough for the target market?
- Does the vacancy show what the company offers beyond generic words?
- Does it describe real constraints honestly?
- Does it sound like the team actually speaks?
- Are there anti-selling elements: manipulative phrases, excessive demands, unpaid tasks, vague growth promises?
- Are target channels named: job boards, Telegram channels, professional communities, referrals, direct search?

## Role Scorecard

Use a 1-5 scale with evidence for each criterion:

- Business outcomes: can deliver the target result.
- Hard skills: demonstrated technical/professional ability.
- Sufficient competence: enough skill for the current task without overhiring.
- Learning ability: can adapt to company-specific context.
- Motivation: wants the role for reasons aligned with the work.
- Soft skills: communication, reliability, feedback, ownership.
- Values fit: behavior does not violate team culture and ethics.
- Risk flags: contradictions, unrealistic expectations, toxicity, weak evidence, compensation mismatch.

## Interview Structure

1. Context and warm start: reduce stress and explain the conversation.
2. Candidate story: career path, decisions, achievements, failures.
3. Evidence block: ask for examples, artifacts, numbers, cases, references.
4. Role-specific challenge: practical questions or paid test task when needed.
5. Motivation and values: why this role, what matters, what environment works.
6. Mutual expectations: compensation, schedule, management style, growth.
7. Close: next steps, risks to verify, open questions.

## Onboarding And Compliance Prompts

- Employment format must be checked with legal/accounting when uncertain: labor contract, civil contract, contractor, self-employed, individual entrepreneur.
- Avoid labor-law terms in contractor documents when the relationship is not employment.
- Documents, reporting deadlines, salary dates, probation terms, and role-specific documents vary by jurisdiction and role.
- The assistant may provide a checklist and risk prompts, but must not present itself as legal advice.
